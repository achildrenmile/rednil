The EU AI Act is in force. The regulation has been effective since August 2024, transition periods are running, and from August 2026 most obligations for operators of AI systems will apply. For tech corporations with their own legal departments, this is a challenge, but not an existential one. For small and medium enterprises in the DACH region, the picture looks different: if you do not have an in-house compliance team, you are facing a regulatory framework that seems overwhelming at first glance.

The good news: for most SMEs, the actual effort is more manageable than the headlines suggest. The bad news: manageable does not mean zero.

## Who is actually affected?

The EU AI Act distinguishes between providers (who develop or place AI systems on the market) and deployers (who use AI systems). Most SMEs are deployers. They do not build their own models; they use tools: an AI-powered CRM, automated invoice processing, a chatbot in customer service.

<Callout type="info" title="Key question">
  The right question is not "Are we using AI?" but rather: "Are we deploying a system that falls under the EU AI Act definition, and if so, in which risk category?"
</Callout>

The risk categories of the EU AI Act are at the heart of the regulation:

- **Prohibited practices:** Social scoring, subliminal manipulation, real-time remote biometric identification in public spaces (with exceptions for law enforcement). Typically irrelevant for SMEs.
- **High risk:** AI systems in areas such as employment (personnel selection!), creditworthiness, education, critical infrastructure. This is where the main compliance effort lies for SMEs.
- **Limited risk:** Systems with transparency obligations, e.g. chatbots must be identifiable as such.
- **Minimal risk:** Everything else. No special obligations.

## Three concrete steps for 2026

### 1. Inventory: What are we using?

Sounds trivial, but it is the most important step. Create a list of all AI-based tools in your company. This includes not only obvious systems like ChatGPT integrations, but also embedded AI in existing software: automated suggestions in your ERP, AI-powered spam filters in your email solution, image recognition in your quality control.

For each system: Who is the provider? What data goes in? What decisions or recommendations come out? Are people affected (employees, customers)?

### 2. Risk assessment: Where do we stand?

Assign each system to a risk category. The critical question: are you using AI in recruitment? In credit scoring? In safety-critical processes? If yes, the high-risk requirements apply: risk management system, data governance, technical documentation, human oversight, accuracy and robustness.

If not, and this applies to the majority of SMEs, your obligations are limited to transparency and basic understanding.

### 3. Documentation and governance

Even for minimal-risk systems: you should document which AI systems you deploy, who is internally responsible, and what usage policies apply. This is not a bureaucratic exercise but risk management. If an AI system makes a flawed decision, you want to know who is in charge and what processes kick in.

<Callout type="warning" title="Note">
  Switzerland is not directly subject to the EU AI Act, but Swiss companies providing services to EU customers or deploying AI systems on the EU market fall under the regulation. Similar to GDPR.
</Callout>

## What business leaders do not need to do

- **Certify your own AI models.** You are a deployer, not a provider. Certification is the provider's responsibility.
- **Declare every spreadsheet formula as AI.** The AI Act defines AI systems specifically. Rule-based automation typically does not fall under it.
- **Hire an AI compliance officer.** Not yet. But having a designated responsible person is sensible.
- **Panic.** The regulation is demanding, but doable. If you survived GDPR, you will survive the AI Act.

## Next steps

The pragmatic approach: start with the inventory. Today. Not next month, not after the next strategy meeting. A simple spreadsheet with columns for *System*, *Provider*, *Purpose*, *Affected persons*, *Risk category* is enough to begin with. On that basis, you can decide whether you need external advice, or whether your actual compliance effort is smaller than feared.
